# Synthesize Research with Provenance

Answer the decision behind the question, while keeping every important claim traceable.

## Frame

State the question, decision, scope, date sensitivity, geography, definitions, and evidence threshold. Break broad questions into claim-sized subquestions.

## Source deliberately

Prefer:

1. primary documentation, standards, laws, datasets, repositories, papers, and direct vendor material;
2. strong independent analysis for comparison and failure reports;
3. community evidence for lived experience, clearly labeled.

For technical questions, verify against official documentation and source repositories. For high-stakes claims, seek independent corroboration. Check publication date and the date the underlying event or data occurred.

## Build a claim ledger

For each material claim record:

- claim;
- source;
- evidence type;
- publication or observation date;
- scope and caveats;
- confidence;
- contradiction or missing evidence.

Distinguish quotation, paraphrase, calculation, and inference. Follow source quotation limits and do not use search snippets as final evidence when the underlying page is available.

## Output

Lead with the conclusion and confidence. Then give the evidence, alternatives, important unknowns, and direct source links beside the claims they support. If evidence is stale or inaccessible, say so explicitly and propose the cheapest refresh.
