# Simulate Real User Journeys

Use simulated users to generate hypotheses and test coverage, never as a substitute for actual user research.

## Ground the users

Derive roles from real product evidence, support history, domain constraints, or the stated audience. Avoid demographic fiction that does not change behavior. Vary meaningful conditions:

- novice versus expert;
- hurried versus exploratory;
- low trust versus high trust;
- keyboard, zoom, screen-reader, or reduced-motion needs;
- weak connectivity or old data;
- mistaken mental model;
- adversarial or boundary-pushing intent;
- operator responsible for recovery.

Label each assumption as confirmed, provisional, or unknown.

## Walk the journey

For every simulated user:

1. state their goal, prior knowledge, constraints, and success signal;
2. begin from the screen or entry point they would actually have;
3. narrate what is visible, what they infer, and the next action;
4. record hesitation, ambiguity, wrong turns, invisible state, and trust questions;
5. introduce one realistic failure;
6. test correction, undo, resume, and help;
7. judge the end state from the user's outcome, not system completion.

Use actual UI, artifacts, or behavior where possible. Do not invent capabilities to keep the story moving.

## Synthesize

Separate:

- observed interface fact;
- simulated reaction;
- design hypothesis;
- research question.

Look for friction shared across roles and severe failures affecting one important role.

## Output

Provide journey traces, friction moments, recovery gaps, design opportunities, and a short real-user research plan to validate the highest-risk hypotheses.
