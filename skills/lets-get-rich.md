# Let's Get Rich: Convert Built Capability Into Revenue

Shipped capability is not revenue, and more capability rarely becomes revenue by itself. The gap is almost never the code — it is that no identified person, at an identified moment, is already spending money on this problem. Work that gap directly.

This skill starts *after* something exists. To decide what to build in the first place, use `opportunity-discovery`; loading both is fine, but do not let ideation stand in for a buyer.

## Frame the asset honestly

Inventory what could be charged for, separating:

- **capability**: what the thing does that is hard to do otherwise;
- **artifact**: output someone would want even if they never touch the tool;
- **taste**: judgment encoded in it that a buyer cannot easily reproduce;
- **distribution**: the attention, audience, reputation, or relationships already held.

Distribution is usually the binding constraint, not capability. A builder with six projects and no audience has a distribution problem wearing a product costume. Name which of the four is scarcest here, because that is the one to work on.

## Find the buyer, not the market

A market is an abstraction; a buyer is a person with a budget. Look for a moment where money already moves:

- who currently pays a human, a consultancy, or a subscription to do this badly?
- what does the failure cost them per week, in money, risk, or headcount?
- which budget line does it come out of, and who signs?
- what are they doing *instead* right now — the honest competitor is usually a spreadsheet, an intern, or nothing.

If no money currently moves against this problem, the sale requires creating a budget. That is a longer, more expensive path — say so out loud rather than assuming.

## Choose the mechanism

Pick by evidence already in hand, not by preference:

- **tool/licence** — many buyers, shallow need, low touch. Needs distribution to work at all.
- **service** — few buyers, deep need, high touch. Fastest to first revenue; caps at your hours.
- **productised service** — a repeated engagement with a fixed scope and price. Usually the correct first step, because it earns money *while* discovering what to build.
- **artifact/output** — sell the result, not the machine. Works when the buyer wants the answer, not the tool.
- **platform/marketplace** — cross-side value. Needs both sides at once; do not start here.

Decision rule: if there is no distribution, do not choose a mechanism that requires distribution. Sell high-touch first, and buy the audience with the proceeds.

## Price on delivered value

Cost-plus and hourly pricing both cap earnings at effort. Price against the outcome:

- anchor to what the failure costs them, or to what they pay today for the worse option;
- charge for the decision, the risk removed, or the time reclaimed;
- if a price is easy to say without flinching, it is too low;
- raise it until you lose roughly one deal in four; the first "yes" with no hesitation is a data point that the number is wrong.

Different buyers get different value from identical work. Segment by value received, never by effort spent.

## Test willingness to pay

Enthusiasm is not evidence. Only these count:

- money paid, or a signed order;
- prepayment or a deposit;
- a scheduled contract, procurement step, or security review started;
- a cost incurred to switch — data migrated, a workflow changed, a person assigned.

Praise, sign-ups, stars, a "definitely would use this", and a warm intro are all zero. Design the smallest test that produces one real signal in under two weeks — usually an offer made to a named person at a real price, not a build.

State plainly which signals exist and which are absent. A plan resting on zero real signals is a hypothesis, and should be labelled one.

## Guardrails

- Do not let "one more feature" substitute for an offer to a named person; that is the most common failure, and it feels like progress.
- Do not model revenue from percentages of a market size. Build it from named buyers × price × close rate.
- Do not recommend anything requiring capital, licensing, or risk the user has not said they will take.
- No get-rich-quick mechanics, no schemes that need a greater fool, no advice that is really unlicensed financial, legal, or tax advice — say when a professional is needed.
- If the honest answer is that this project has no buyer, say that. A clear no is worth more than a plan.

## Output

Return:

1. the asset inventory, with the scarcest of the four named;
2. two or three candidate buyers, each with the moment, the budget line, and the current alternative;
3. the recommended mechanism and why the alternatives lose here;
4. a price with the value anchor it is derived from;
5. the smallest test that yields a real willingness-to-pay signal, with its deadline;
6. the evidence that exists versus the assumptions being made;
7. the kill criterion — what result would mean stop.
